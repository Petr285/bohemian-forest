Bohemian Forest

The Bohemian Forest, known in Czech as Šumava, stretches for more than 120 kilometers along the border between the Czech Republic, Germany, and Austria. This ancient mountain range, covered by deep spruce and beech forests, forms one of the largest continuous natural areas in Central Europe. It is a land where mist rises from endless woods at dawn, rivers carve their way through silent valleys, and the air carries the scent of pine and moss.

Much of the region is protected as the Šumava National Park, which together with Germany’s Bavarian Forest National Park forms a vast cross-border wilderness known as the “Green Roof of Europe.” It is a rare refuge for many endangered species, including the Eurasian lynx, black stork, and capercaillie. Here, nature has been allowed to follow its own rhythm, and fallen trees, peat bogs, and clear glacial lakes tell stories of centuries past.

The Bohemian Forest is not only a sanctuary for wildlife, but also a place of quiet human history. Scattered wooden cottages, forgotten chapels, and the remains of old glassworks and border villages remind visitors of a time when life here was shaped by the forest itself. Today, these traces of the past blend harmoniously with nature’s slow renewal.

Visitors can enjoy a network of hiking and cycling trails that lead through forests, meadows, and mountain ridges offering panoramic views of the Vltava River valley and distant Alps. In winter, Šumava turns into a peaceful white landscape, ideal for cross-country skiing and winter hiking, where silence is broken only by the crunch of snow underfoot.

The Bohemian Forest is a place to breathe, to pause, and to rediscover the simple rhythm of nature. Whether you seek adventure or serenity, sunrise over a misty lake or the whisper of wind through the trees, Šumava offers a timeless invitation — to slow down, to listen, and to feel part of something greater than yourself.
