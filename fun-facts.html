Bohemian Forest Fun Facts

  • The Plechý mountain (1,378 m) is the highest peak on the Czech side of the Bohemian Forest.
  • The Plešné Lake is a glacial lake located directly beneath Plechý and is one of only a few of its kind in the Czech Republic.
  • The Boubín Virgin Forest (Boubínský prales) is one of the oldest natural forests in Europe, protected since 1858.
  • The Vltava River, which flows through Prague, has its source near Kvilda, a small mountain village in Šumava.
  • During the Cold War, large parts of Šumava were a restricted border zone, which helped preserve its wild nature.
  • The region is home to unique peat bogs, some over 8,000 years old, and many are now protected as nature reserves.
  • Šumava and the Bavarian Forest together form a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve.
  • Locals often say that Šumava has its own “microclimate” — misty mornings, sudden sunshine, and long winters.
  • The Bohemian Forest plateau is so flat in places that rainwater can flow either north to the North Sea or south to the Black Sea.
  • The area is often called the “Green Roof of Europe”, thanks to its high elevation and endless spruce forests.
  • The oldest wooden church in the region, St. Stephen’s Chapel near Kvilda, dates back to the early 18th century.
  • Brown bears used to roam Šumava until the late 19th century — now, they occasionally wander over from Slovakia or the Carpathians.
  • The Sumava National Park is home to over 1,200 species of plants and 300 species of vertebrates.
  • The famous Poledník Lookout Tower was originally built as a Cold War military radar post.
  • Traditional wooden houses in Šumava have a distinctive Alpine style, often with stone bases and painted shutters.
  • In some villages like Kvilda and Horská Kvilda, temperatures can drop below –30 °C, making them the coldest places in the Czech Republic.
  • The Laka Lake (Jezero Laka) is the smallest and shallowest glacial lake in the Bohemian Forest — it’s known for floating islands of peat.
  • In local folklore, Šumava is sometimes called “the land of ghosts and fog”, inspired by its mysterious mists and legends of lost travelers.
